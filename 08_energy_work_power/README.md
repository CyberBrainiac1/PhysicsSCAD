# Energy Landscape Board

Model: `energy_landscape_board.scad`

## What This Is
Track-profile style board for comparing height, potential energy, and motion outcomes.

## What It Teaches
Energy conservation intuition, path comparisons, and loss discussions.

## Why This Matters
Energy methods simplify many mechanics problems when force details are messy.

## How To Print
Print profile and marker pieces flat. Consider thicker base for repeated classroom handling.

## Quick Start
Place two start markers at different heights and predict bottom speed ordering.

## How to Learn With This
### Student mode
- Use “height map first” before writing equations.

### Mentor mode
- Ask “what is conserved, and under what assumption?”

### Hands-on learning tasks
1. Compare high-start and low-start cases.
2. Compare two paths with same start/end heights.
3. Mark points where kinetic energy should be largest/smallest.
4. Add “loss zones” to discuss non-conservative effects.
5. Build one case where energy method beats force decomposition.

## Things to Predict Before Using
- Which point has maximum potential energy?
- If start/end heights match, what can you predict about speed (idealized)?
- Where do losses matter most in real setups?

## Challenge Prompts
- Design two tracks with equal height drop but different expected real-world final speeds.
- Explain when work-energy is preferable to Newton’s 2nd law component-by-component.

## Explanation Prompts
- Why does path shape alone not set final speed in ideal conservative cases?
- Why is “energy lost” really transfer to thermal/sound/internal forms?

## Common Mistakes / Misconceptions
- Assuming steepest segment always means greatest final speed.
- Treating potential energy as absolute instead of reference-dependent.
- Ignoring non-conservative work in real systems.

## Physics 1 Connection
Work-energy theorem, conservation of energy, power discussions.

## F=ma Connection

Battery budget, mechanism efficiency, and transfer losses in drivetrains and shooters.
